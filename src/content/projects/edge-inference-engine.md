## Overview

Most deepfake videos fail in the same way: the audio and the lips don't quite match. Synthesis models that generate convincing individual frames tend to lose coherence when integrated over time — the audio transcript reconstructed from the video's lip motion deviates from the actual audio track in ways that are detectable, even if imperceptible to a casual viewer. This project built a pipeline to detect and quantify that mismatch.

## Pipeline Architecture

The detection system is a three-stage pipeline. First, a Conformer — the audio-specialized transformer variant originally developed for speech recognition — transcribes the audio track into a word sequence. Second, 2D ResNet-18 extracts per-frame visual features, which are passed to Wav2Lip to reconstruct what the lip motion in the video implies the spoken words should be. Third, a transformer decoder computes the word error rate (WER) between the audio transcript and the lip-motion reconstruction.

Literature on deepfake video synthesis suggests that manipulated content exhibits WER above 20% due to the difficulty of maintaining tight audio-visual synchrony across the full duration of a clip. We set the detection threshold at 40% WER — conservative enough to tolerate legitimate model errors from both the transcription and lip reconstruction stages while still flagging clear manipulations.

## Evaluation and Results

The pipeline was evaluated on a held-out set of genuine and synthesized video content. The 40% WER threshold provided robust separation between the two classes, with false positive rates within acceptable bounds for a media integrity application.

The research was submitted to the IEEE International Conference on Advances in Data Engineering and Intelligent Systems (CADEIS 2024) and was selected as one of 130 accepted papers from 750 submissions. The work received the Best Paper Award (Runner-Up) at the conference — recognition that felt less like validation of the result than of the problem framing: that deepfake detection, approached correctly, is a synchronization problem before it is a visual one.

## Reflections

The most interesting part of this project was what it revealed about the limits of the approach. The pipeline works well on content generated by current synthesis methods, which prioritize visual quality and accept audio-visual drift as an acceptable tradeoff. A synthesis model specifically optimized to minimize WER would defeat the detector. That arms-race dynamic — between synthesis and detection, each improving the other — is why media integrity remains an open problem rather than a solved one.
