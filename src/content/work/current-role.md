Product work at Siam Computing was my first extended exposure to the full lifecycle between an insight and a shipped feature. The company was building HRM systems for healthcare, a domain where the gap between what a product should theoretically do and what clinical staff will actually adopt is wide and unforgiving.

My work centered on authoring five comprehensive product documents — PRDs, feature requirements, competitive analysis — covering more than 25 distinct HRM features. The research phase was the more interesting half: conducting interviews with over 25 healthcare providers and analyzing 1,000+ user sessions in Mixpanel to identify where workflows broke down. The data pointed clearly to appointment booking as the highest-friction surface, and the product changes that followed reduced booking time by 2.4 minutes per interaction — meaningful at scale across a clinical workforce.

Managing stakeholders across medical staff, the engineering team, and C-suite leadership in parallel was its own discipline. The clearest lesson: the most technically correct product spec fails if the people who have to act on it do not share the same model of the problem. I left Siam Computing with a sharper instinct for that translation layer, and a stronger belief that good ML engineering requires the same skill — you cannot deploy a model responsibly without understanding what the people using it actually need.
